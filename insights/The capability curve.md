# The capability curve

## Executive Summary
This session explores the "Capability Curve" in the context of AI development—specifically how coordinated AI systems lower the cost of influence and how research and productization compound over time. The talk addresses the transition from individual models to sophisticated, multi-layered agentic systems that can handle increasingly complex engineering and business tasks.

## Key Points
- **The P-Q Envelope (Technical Analogy):** The session uses the engineering concept of a generator's capability curve ($P$ for active power, $Q$ for reactive power) as an analogy for AI systems. Just as a generator has safe operating boundaries (stator current, rotor limits, stability), an AI agent has boundaries defined by its context window, reasoning depth, and tool reliability.
- **Compounding Capabilities:** Progress in AI is not linear; it is compounding. Success in research (better models) leads to better productization (better tools like Claude Code), which in turn accelerates further research.
- **Lowering the Cost of Influence:** As model intelligence scales, the human effort required to "influence" or direct a complex software project decreases. One "Agent Orchestrator" can now achieve what previously required a full engineering pod.
- **Operational Boundaries:**
    - **Stator Limit (Context):** The total volume of information an agent can process safely.
    - **Rotor/Stability Limit (Reasoning):** The depth of logic an agent can maintain without losing the "thread" or becoming unstable (hallucinating).
- **Under-excitation (Agent Passivity):** The risk of an agent withholding too much information or failing to act when it should (addressing "silent saturation" or over-patching).

## Notable Quotes
- "Software is becoming something you ask for instead of something you hire for."
- "Coordinated AI systems are fundamentally lowering the cost of influence across every vertical."
- "The real skill for 2026 is managing the 'envelope'—knowing where the agent is safe to run autonomously and where it needs a human stabilizer."

## Takeaways
- **Shift from Coder to Orchestrator:** Focus on defining the "envelope" (constraints and rubrics) within which agents can operate.
- **Monitor the Stability Limit:** Use rigorous evaluations to identify the point where an agent's reasoning "breaks" under high complexity.
- **Invest in Compounding Tools:** Build internal tools and "Skills" that allow your team to benefit from each new model jump without rebuilding your entire architecture.
- **Avoid "Under-excitation":** Regularly audit your prompts to ensure that defensive "scaffolding" isn't preventing the agent from utilizing its full reasoning capability.
